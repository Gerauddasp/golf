# Video 81: Is Your Trail Elbow Doing THIS in the Downswing?

**YouTube ID**: ojgf12oXyB8

**Upload Date**: Aug 9, 2025

## Overview

This video examines common instruction regarding trail elbow position during the downswing through biomechanical analysis using a unique bird's-eye camera angle. AMG debunks popular advice about "leading with the trail elbow," "driving the elbow in front of the navel," or "skipping a stone" by demonstrating what actually happens in elite players' swings. Using overhead tracking perpendicular to the pelvis with a visual pelvis box reference system, the analysis reveals that a major champion's trail elbow moves progressively deeper and farther behind the pelvis during the downswing—the opposite of conventional teaching.

## Key Concepts

### Bird's-Eye Analysis Method
- **Camera position**: 90° perpendicular to pelvis, directly overhead
- **Pelvis box reference**: Visual aid that remains square to and aligned with front of pelvis
- **Purpose**: Shows what moves in front of player regardless of body rotation
- **Tracking marker**: Blue dot on trail elbow, red dot on trail hip
- **Advantage**: Eliminates visual distortion from standard camera angles

### Backswing Trail Elbow Movement
- **At address**: Trail elbow positioned in front of pelvis (as expected)
- **Shaft parallel (backswing)**: Elbow already moved well outside pelvis box
- **Cause**: Shoulders rotating approximately 30° more than pelvis
- **Lead arm parallel**: Elbow continues drifting away from pelvis
- **At top**: Elbow nearly as deep as right hip
- **Key point**: Progressive separation during backswing sets stage for downswing

### Downswing Trail Elbow Reality
- **Lead arm parallel (downswing)**: Elbow positioned deeper and farther behind right hip
- **Mechanism**: Trail arm lowers while pelvis rotates
- **Shoulder-pelvis relationship**: Maintains same close relationship from top
- **Mid-downswing**: Elbow shifts even farther away and behind hip
- **Critical finding**: Movement is literally opposite of common instruction
- **Implication**: "Drive elbow in front" cues contradict elite biomechanics

### Common Instruction vs. Reality
- **Popular cues debunked**:
  - "Lead the downswing with your trail elbow"
  - "Drive your elbow in front of your navel"
  - "Skip a stone" motion
  - "Get your elbow in front"
- **Actual elite pattern**: Trail elbow moves progressively deeper behind pelvis
- **Why instruction persists**: Traditional camera angles create visual illusions
- **Teaching implication**: Overhead perspective reveals true movement patterns

## Biomechanical Analysis

### Shoulder-Pelvis Differential
- **Backswing**: Shoulders rotate ~30° more than pelvis
- **Result**: Trail elbow moves outside pelvis box early
- **Downswing**: Shoulders maintain close relationship with pelvis from top
- **Effect**: As pelvis rotates, elbow position relative to pelvis stays deep/behind

### Trail Arm Lowering Mechanics
- **Action**: Trail arm lowers during early downswing
- **Timing**: Occurs simultaneously with pelvis rotation
- **Result**: Elbow drops down and stays behind rotating pelvis
- **Contrast**: Not moving forward/in front as commonly taught

### Pelvis Box Reference System
- **Function**: Provides stable coordinate system relative to body
- **Benefit**: Shows true position independent of body rotation
- **Key insight**: What appears "in front" from side view may be "behind" from overhead
- **Application**: Reveals hidden aspects of 3D movement patterns

## Key Takeaways

1. **Elite trail elbow position during downswing moves progressively deeper and farther behind the pelvis, not in front of it**

2. **The trail elbow's depth is established during backswing through shoulder-pelvis separation (approximately 30° differential)**

3. **During downswing, the trail arm lowers while maintaining its deep position relative to the rotating pelvis**

4. **Common instruction cues like "lead with the elbow" or "skip a stone" contradict what actually happens in elite swings**

## Application for Players

### Self-Assessment
- **Record overhead video**: Position camera perpendicular to target line, above your position
- **Check backswing**: Does trail elbow move outside body early due to shoulder turn?
- **Analyze downswing**: Does elbow stay deep/behind or drive forward?
- **Compare feels**: What you feel vs. what actually happens may differ significantly

### Practice Focus
- **Stop forcing elbow forward**: Let trail arm lower naturally during transition
- **Maintain depth**: Allow elbow to stay behind as body rotates
- **Rotation-dominant**: Focus on pelvis and thorax rotation, not arm manipulation
- **Trust the pattern**: Elite movements may feel "wrong" if you've trained opposite pattern

### Red Flags
- **Feeling stuck**: May indicate trying to drive elbow forward
- **Loss of power**: Forward elbow drive can reduce effective sequencing
- **Inconsistent contact**: Manipulating elbow position disrupts natural path
- **Chicken wing**: Ironically, trying to get elbow "in front" can cause lead arm breakdown

## Summary

This biomechanical analysis definitively shows that elite players' trail elbows move deeper and farther behind the pelvis during the downswing, contradicting popular instruction cues about "leading with the elbow" or "driving the elbow in front of the navel." The overhead camera perspective, positioned perpendicular to the pelvis with a pelvis box reference system, reveals movement patterns invisible from traditional angles. The trail arm lowers during transition while maintaining its deep position relative to the rotating pelvis—a pattern established during the backswing through approximately 30° of shoulder-pelvis separation. This finding challenges fundamental assumptions in golf instruction and demonstrates the importance of 3D motion analysis over 2D visual interpretation.
