Each drill targets a specific dimension to help golfers achieve pure contact and increased distance.

## Key Concepts

### The Physics of Great Ball Strike

To hit the ball consistently in the sweet spot, golfers must understand three critical dimensions:

1. **X-Axis (Horizontal Low Point)**
   - The low point should be at the ball or slightly beyond it
   - Creates a descending blow on the ball
   - Gets the leading edge underneath the equator of the ball
   - If low point is before the ball: results in fat and thin shots
   - If low point is at or beyond the ball: creates pure strike

2. **Y-Axis (Vertical Low Point)**
   - Controls how high or low the club head comes into the ball
   - Requires maintaining proper posture throughout the swing
   - Loss of posture is one of the most common problems Andrew sees

3. **Z-Axis (Distance from Body)**
   - Controls how far the club is from your body during impact
   - Critical for avoiding shanks (hitting the hosel)
   - Especially important on shorter, three-quarter shots

## The Four Drills

### Drill #1: Feet Together Drill (X-Axis Control)

**Purpose:** Control horizontal low point and prevent excessive lateral movement

**Execution:**
- Stage 1: Place feet together and make swings without a ball
- Feel being very central to the ball position
- You cannot move too far off center or you'll fall over
- The low point will naturally be between the sternum and left shoulder
- Stage 2: Increase swing speed while maintaining stability
- Body remains stable, allowing hands and arms to create speed
- Get more leverage and rotation as the right arm straightens

**Key Points:**
- The horizontal low point is opposite where the sternum is
- Low point ranges from sternum to under the left shoulder (deepest part of divot)
- This drill centralizes your position relative to the ball
- Can be used not just for pure ball strike but also to increase speed
- Many golfers are surprised by how well they strike the ball with feet together

**Common Result:** Beautiful pure ball strike because you're not moving off center

---

### Drill #2: Stomp Drill (X-Axis Control - Advanced)

**Purpose:** Move low point forward and prevent hanging back on the ball

**Problem Addressed:**
- Many amateur golfers try to "help" the ball up in the air
- This causes the horizontal low point to move further behind the ball
- Results in poor contact and loss of distance

**Execution:**
- Start in slow motion until you get the coordination
- As you finish the backswing, lift the left foot off the ground
- Stomp it back down as you start the downswing
- The stomp doesn't have to be a step forward
- Focus on loading the left side
- Loading the left side brings the low point forward
- The sternum moves left relative to the ball
- This moves along the X-axis, bringing low point further forward

**Power Benefits:**
- Creates lateral force (moving left)
- Creates vertical force (loading into the ground)
- Creates rotational force (body turn)
- All three forces combine in one powerful move

**Key Insight:** The most important aspect is the influence on the horizontal low point, ensuring it comes after the ball

---

### Drill #3: Tee to Right Pocket Drill (Y-Axis Control)

**Purpose:** Maintain proper posture throughout the backswing and control vertical axis

**Problem Addressed:**
- Most common problem: losing control of posture on the backswing
- Club starts too flat with hands going behind shoulders
- This brings the left shoulder too high
- Flat backswing pulls posture out of position (both right and up)
- Head gets pulled up and right, creating vertical and horizontal axis problems

**Execution:**
- Hold a tee in your grip
- Point the tee towards your right pocket throughout the backswing
- Keep tee pointing to right pocket at takeaway, halfway back, and three-quarters back
- This creates room for the left shoulder to come underneath the chin
- Left shoulder goes down, down, down (not up)
- Maintains original height throughout the backswing
- Prevents being knocked out of original posture position

**Key Benefit:** Much easier to control height on the backswing and maintain the Y-axis for great ball strike

**Why It Works:** When the tee points to the right pocket, there's room for the left shoulder to move properly under the chin without disrupting posture

---

### Drill #4: Head Cover Drill (Z-Axis Control)

**Purpose:** Control distance of club from body and prevent shanks

**Setup:**
- Place a head cover (or sponge) about a ball's width outside the ball
- Focus on three-quarter or half shots (where most shanks occur)

**Problem Addressed:**
- On shorter shots, arms become very lively while the body is lazy
- Instead of turning, golfers just use their arms
- Left arm comes on top of the rib cage and stays there
- Arm is now closer to the ball
- This means the hosel (socket) is closer to the ball
- Results in shanks and loss of Z-axis control

**Execution:**
- Try to hit the ball while missing the head cover
- This instinctively trains proper Z-axis control
- Forces you to keep the club at the proper distance from your body
- Promotes body turn rather than just arm swing
- Prevents the left arm from getting trapped on top of the rib cage

**Key Understanding:** The head cover provides immediate feedback and forces you to maintain proper club path and distance from your body

## Bonus Tip: Face Tape Feedback

**Tool:** Impact tape (or foot spray, though Andrew finds it messier)

**Purpose:**
- Provides honest feedback about strike patterns
- Shows tendencies: toe, heel, high, low hits
- Helps identify which drill (X, Y, or Z axis) you should prioritize
- "The face tape doesn't lie"

**Sweet Spot Location:**
- Usually around the 4th or 5th groove
- Can vary by manufacturer and club type
- Can find it by bouncing a ball on the club face and listening for the purest sound

**Application:**
- Hit multiple shots with tape on the face
- Analyze the pattern
- Determine which axis needs the most work
- Focus on the drill that addresses your specific tendency
- Fine-tune your strike pattern through targeted practice

**Andrew's Results:** Both demonstration shots were middle of the club face, showing consistency when applying these principles

## Common Problems Addressed

1. **Fat Shots ("Chunky Monkey")** - Low point too early (before ball)
2. **Thin Shots ("Thin as my granny")** - Hitting above the equator of the ball
3. **Inconsistent Contact** - Strikes all over the club face
4. **Short Shots** - Coming up short of target due to poor contact
5. **Loose Contact** - Lack of solid feel at impact
6. **Shanks** - Hitting off the hosel (Z-axis problem)
7. **Hanging Back** - Trying to lift the ball, moving low point backward

## Key Teaching Points

### For X-Axis (Horizontal Low Point):
- Sternum position relative to ball is critical
- Low point ranges from sternum to left shoulder
- Descending blow is essential for pure contact
- Loading the left side moves low point forward
- Don't try to "help" the ball up

### For Y-Axis (Vertical Control):
- Maintain original height throughout backswing
- Left shoulder should go DOWN, not up
- Flat takeaway is a common error
- Proper shoulder plane prevents pulling out of posture
- Chin position creates space for proper shoulder turn

### For Z-Axis (Distance Control):
- Body must turn, not just arms swing
- Left arm needs to stay connected to body rotation
- Especially critical on shorter, controlled shots
- Physical obstacle (head cover) provides instant feedback
- Prevents arm-only swing pattern

## Practice Recommendations

1. **Start with Feet Together Drill**
   - Establishes basic centering and X-axis control
   - Build confidence with pure strikes
   - Progress to adding speed while maintaining balance

2. **Add Stomp Drill for Power**
   - Reinforces forward low point
   - Adds power components (lateral, vertical, rotational force)
   - Great for players who hang back

3. **Use Tee Drill for Posture**
   - Critical for maintaining height
   - Fixes flat backswing issues
   - Repeat frequently to ingrain proper shoulder plane

4. **Apply Head Cover Drill for Precision**
   - Essential for shank-prone players
   - Excellent for three-quarter and half shots
   - Promotes body turn over arm swing

5. **Regular Face Tape Testing**
   - Provides objective feedback
   - Identifies which drill to prioritize
   - Tracks improvement over time
   - Reveals tendencies before they become habits
