## Overview
This video is part of Andrew Cullen's chipping series (Step 2) and focuses on simplifying the chipping motion by using ONE moving part: the chest/rib cage rotation. The lesson emphasizes body-driven chipping over hand-and-arm manipulation for consistent, repeatable results.

---

## Key Concepts

### The One Moving Part Philosophy
- **Primary Movement:** Chest/rib cage rotation only
- **Arms Feel:** As if they're "stuck onto my sides"
- **Distance Control:** Short shot = short turn, longer shot = longer turn
- **Result:** Where the body goes, the hands, arms, and club follow

### Proper Setup Requirements
- **Posture:** Same good posture as the full swing
- **Why It Matters:** Good posture allows the body turn to naturally send the club down toward the ball
- **Grip:** Same grip as used in the full swing
- **Body Position:** Tilted forward from the hips (not upright)

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### The "Windscreen Wipers" Shot
**What It Is:**
- Bad posture (very upright, shoulders rounded, head down)
- Swing controlled by hands and arms doing their own thing
- Arms moving independently without body rotation

**Why It Fails:**
- Produces occasional good shots but very difficult to repeat
- Lacks consistency in both movement and ball reaction
- No reliable pattern for distance control

**The Solution:**
- Turn the chest back and through from good posture
- Keep arms connected to the body turn
- Focus on repetition of movement = repetition of ball reaction

---

## The Technique Step-by-Step

1. **Setup:** Establish good posture (same as full swing)
2. **Grip:** Use your normal full swing grip
3. **Mental Image:** Feel arms stuck to your sides
4. **Backswing:** Turn chest/rib cage back (amount depends on distance needed)
5. **Through Swing:** Turn chest/rib cage through
6. **Contact:** Collect the ball on the way past
7. **Result:** Ball sent in the right direction with consistent strike

---

## Core Principles

### Repetition is Key
- Repetition of movement leads to repetition of ball reaction
- Consistency comes from simplifying to one moving part
- Body rotation is more repeatable than hand/arm manipulation

### Distance Control
- Controlled entirely by the amount of chest turn
- More turn = longer shot
- Less turn = shorter shot
- No need to manipulate hands or arms

### Connection
- Arms stay connected to the body throughout
- Where the body goes, everything follows
- Creates a unified, simple motion

## Key Takeaway
**Simplify chipping to ONE moving part: the chest turn.** Use the same good posture and grip as your full swing, keep your arms connected to your body, and let the rotation of your rib cage control the entire motion. Short turn for short chips, longer turn for longer chips. This body-driven approach creates consistency and repeatability that hand-and-arm manipulation cannot achieve.
